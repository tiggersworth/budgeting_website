Signs that you are spending too much


  1. You carry credit card debt. Thirty-four percent of households admit to carrying credit card debt month to month. If you’re not paying off your credit card balance completely at the end of the month, you’re probably spending more than you should. Without a doubt, you’re paying unnecessary interest payments. (See Expert Tips For Cutting Credit Card Debt and 6 Major Credit Card Mistakes.)

  2. You have no retirement savings. The problem is getting worse. About one third of working-age Americans have no retirement savings. That will spell catastrophe later in life and will force you to work well into your retirement years.

  3. You don’t have enough saved for retirement. The number varies but most financial planners agree that you have to save 10% to 15% of your income each year starting in your 20s. That’s hard to do but it’s essential.

  4. You have too much house. Are you paying more than 28% of your income for housing? If you are, you’re paying too much.

  5. You have too much car. The College Board advises new graduates to spend no more than 15% of their budget on transportation. If you’re a little older than a 20-something college graduate, you might be able to spend a little more but be conservative. Cars depreciate fast!

  6. You aren’t saving for your children’s college education. Student loan debt is a huge problem for today’s recent college graduates. The average graduate will have $33,000 in student loan debt, according to an analysis by financial aid expert Mark Kantrowitz, now publisher at the Edvisors websites. If you aren’t saving now, your children could be saddled with debt at a time where they’re just starting their careers.

  7. You don’t have health insurance. If you don’t have health insurance, you’re asking for financial disaster. If you needed a coronary bypass, you could pay more than $38,500. A serious car accident or long-term care will come with a significantly higher bill. And now, you'll also owe penalties on your income tax for having no health insurance unless you can qualify for an exemption. See Tips on the Health Insurance Marketplace/Exchange.

  8. You eat restaurant food too much. One study found that 1 in 4 people eats fast food every day. Pack your lunch to save one-third to one-half the cost of fast food. Same with coffee. One study found savings as high as 60%.

  9. You don’t have an emergency fund. Experts are all over the map when it comes to the size of your emergency fund. Somewhere between 3 and 12 months’ funds is what you’ll read, but if you have nothing saved for unexpected expenses, don’t worry so much about how many months of expenses you should save – just get started.

  10. You don’t follow the 50/20/30 rule. Half of your budget should go to essentials, 20% to debt payments, retirement and savings contributions and other “financial priorities,” and 30% to lifestyle choices – 300 channels of cable and a brand new cell phone, for example. But this is only one guideline and may not apply to your financial situation. The point is to have financial discipline about how you spend your money. Make a rule of your own or even better, have a trusted financial planner help you make a saving plan
